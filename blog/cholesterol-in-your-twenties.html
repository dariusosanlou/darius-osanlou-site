Health & Longevity

Should you care about
cholesterol in your 20s?

Darius Osanlou, MSc · 15 June 2026 · 6 min read

The thing about heart disease is that it doesn't arrive suddenly. It builds quietly, over decades, in the walls of your arteries — until one day it can't be ignored any more. Which is exactly why most people in their 20s and 30s don't think about it at all.

If you are in your 20s or 30s, you probably feel fine. You almost certainly are fine, in the sense that nothing is hurting you right now. But the biology happening inside your arteries right now is laying the foundation for what shows up in your 40s, 50s and 60s. And the choices you make in this window — around food, around exercise, around body composition — have a disproportionate influence on how that plays out.

This post is about what cholesterol actually is, what it's doing in your body, and why the people who take it seriously early are the ones who don't spend their later years managing the consequences of not having done so.

Why we ignore slow-moving threats

The human brain is extraordinarily good at responding to immediate danger. It is significantly worse at sustaining concern about risks that are 20 or 30 years away. This isn't a character flaw — it's an evolutionary feature. Our nervous systems evolved to respond to threats that could kill us today, not threats that might kill us in three decades.

The result is a predictable pattern: we feel young, we feel well, we have no symptoms, and so we conclude that nothing is wrong. The risk feels abstract in a way that a lion, a car accident or a sharp pain does not. And because the consequences are decades away, the psychological urgency to act never really arrives.

This is one of the central problems I see in the clients I work with. Not ignorance, exactly — most people have a vague sense that diet and exercise matter for long-term health. But vague sense is not the same as felt urgency, and felt urgency is what actually drives behaviour change.

What cholesterol actually is

Cholesterol is not a poison. It is a fat-like substance your body produces and uses constantly — for building cell membranes, synthesising hormones including testosterone and oestrogen, and producing vitamin D. You could not function without it.

The problem is not cholesterol itself. The problem is what happens when there is too much low-density lipoprotein — LDL — circulating in the blood, and when that LDL becomes oxidised.

LDL is the particle that carries cholesterol from the liver to the rest of the body. Think of it as the delivery vehicle. When LDL levels are elevated over a long period, and particularly when the LDL particles are small and dense (which makes them more likely to become oxidised), they begin to penetrate the walls of blood vessels and trigger an inflammatory response. The body attempts to deal with this by sending immune cells to the site. Over time, this process forms what are called plaques — fatty deposits that progressively narrow and stiffen the arteries.

HDL, high-density lipoprotein, works in the opposite direction. It carries cholesterol away from the arteries and back to the liver for disposal. High HDL is broadly protective. Low HDL, combined with high LDL and elevated triglycerides, is when the risk profile becomes genuinely concerning.

When it starts

Here is the part that most people in their 20s don't know, and that I think is worth sitting with: atherosclerosis — the medical term for the build-up of plaques in the arteries — does not begin in middle age. It begins earlier than that. Significantly earlier.

Post-mortem studies on soldiers killed in the Korean and Vietnam Wars, most of them in their early 20s, found early signs of atherosclerotic plaque in a significant proportion of them. More recent research using imaging technology has found early arterial changes in teenagers with elevated LDL or high-risk diets. The disease process that kills people in their 50s and 60s starts laying its foundation decades before any symptom appears.

This is not meant to be alarming for its own sake. It is meant to reframe what "too young to worry about this" actually means. You are not too young to worry about this. You are at the exact age where the choices you make are either building or eroding your future cardiovascular health.

What drives it: the lifestyle factors

Elevated LDL cholesterol and poor cardiovascular markers are not inevitable. They are, in most cases, significantly influenced by lifestyle — and the lifestyle factors that matter most are ones you have direct control over.

Body fat, and particularly visceral fat. Visceral fat — the fat stored around the organs in the abdominal cavity — is metabolically active in ways that subcutaneous fat (the fat just under the skin) is not. It produces inflammatory cytokines that drive the kind of chronic low-grade inflammation that accelerates plaque formation and raises LDL. Reducing visceral fat through calorie control and exercise is one of the most impactful things a person can do for their cardiovascular risk profile.

Exercise. Regular physical activity — both cardiovascular exercise and resistance training — raises HDL, lowers LDL and triglycerides, reduces blood pressure, and improves insulin sensitivity. These are not modest effects. The protective effect of consistent exercise on cardiovascular health is one of the most robustly supported findings in all of medicine. People who exercise regularly simply have better cardiovascular outcomes, across nearly every metric, than people who do not.

Diet quality. This is more nuanced than the old saturated-fat narrative would suggest. The current understanding is that dietary patterns matter more than any single food. Ultra-processed foods, refined carbohydrates in large quantities, and a diet low in vegetables, fibre and healthy fats all contribute to worse lipid profiles and more oxidative stress. Eating with some awareness of food quality — without becoming obsessive about it — moves the needle meaningfully over time.

Sleep and stress. Chronic sleep deprivation and chronic psychological stress both independently worsen cardiovascular markers. Cortisol, the primary stress hormone, raises blood glucose, promotes fat storage around the abdomen, and drives inflammation. If you are consistently under-slept and chronically stressed — which describes a significant proportion of the people I work with — this is not a separate issue from your cardiovascular health. It is part of the same picture.

The argument for acting now

I want to be honest about something. The point of all of this is not to make you afraid. Fear is not a reliable motivator — it tends to produce brief bursts of action followed by avoidance. The research on health behaviour change is clear on this.

The point is to give you an accurate picture of what is actually happening, so you can make an informed decision about what you want to do with it.

The people I know who take their health seriously in their 30s are not doing it from a place of anxiety. They are doing it because they have thought about the kind of life they want to live in their 50s and 60s, and they have worked backwards to what that requires of them now. It is not restriction. It is investment. And like most investments, the earlier you start, the more it compounds.

The alternative — waiting until something goes wrong before paying attention — is a legitimate choice. People make it constantly. I just think most people, if they really thought it through, would not choose it deliberately. They choose it by default, because the urgency never feels real enough to act on.

What to actually do

I am not going to give you a protocol here. That's not what this is. But I will give you the practical starting points that the evidence supports.

Get your bloods checked. A standard lipid panel — total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides — gives you a baseline. If you have not had one done recently, get one. You cannot manage what you are not measuring.

Build consistent exercise into your week. Both types matter. Cardiovascular exercise for the direct effects on lipid profiles and blood pressure; resistance training for the effect on body composition, insulin sensitivity and long-term metabolic health. Three to four sessions a week, done consistently over months and years, produces real and measurable changes in cardiovascular risk.

Reduce visceral fat if you are carrying excess body fat. This is often the single highest-leverage change a person can make. It does not require an aggressive diet or an extreme programme. It requires a moderate, sustained calorie deficit and enough exercise to preserve muscle while losing fat.

Take sleep and stress seriously as health variables. Not as background factors to manage once the real work is done — as central inputs into your cardiovascular health that deserve the same attention as what you eat and how often you train.

The honest summary

You are not invincible. No one who is 25 feels like that is true, but it is. The biology happening in your arteries right now is responsive to what you do. It is building towards something — the question is whether you are paying attention to what that something is.

This does not require you to become obsessive about your health. It requires you to take it seriously enough to make a few consistent choices that your future self will be grateful for. That is a relatively low bar, and it is well within reach of most people who are willing to be honest with themselves about their habits.

The people who do this well are not the ones with the most willpower or the most time. They are the ones who understood early enough that it mattered.
