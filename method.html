The Method

Body psychology.

A coaching approach that treats the mind and body as one problem, not two separate ones.

This isn't therapy, and it isn't a fitness plan. It sits between them and addresses the part that both usually miss.

If information was enough, most people would already have the body they want.

For centuries, medicine, philosophy and fitness have treated mind and body as separate problems requiring separate solutions. You go to a therapist for the mind. You go to a personal trainer for the body. The two rarely speak.

The research tells a different story. Physical practices directly alter psychological outcomes: cortisol levels, attention, mood, affect. Psychological states directly drive physical behaviours: what you eat, how consistently you move, how well you recover from stress. The separation was always artificial, and coaching that ignores it keeps producing the same result: progress, then regression, then starting over.

Body Psychology is built on that insight.

The reason change is so difficult is that every new plan is carried out by the same person, with the same habits, routines, beliefs and patterns that created the problem in the first place. That's why my coaching starts one level deeper.

Before we focus on changing your body, we focus on understanding the person responsible for the decisions that shape it. Your habits. Your mindset. Your environment. Your strengths. Your blind spots.

Because lasting change doesn't come from finding the perfect plan. It comes from becoming the person who can follow one.

Once that foundation is in place, training and nutrition become far more effective. Not because the plan is better, but because the person following it is.

That's what Body Psychology is.

The Four Layers

How the method works.

01

The Body

Training and nutrition built around your goals, lifestyle and health. No template programmes, no banned foods, nothing that falls apart the first time your schedule changes.

02

The Mind

The beliefs, habits and patterns that keep pulling you back to where you started. We surface them, name them, and systematically change the ones that are getting in the way.

03

The Environment

Your work, relationships, stress load, schedule and responsibilities. These aren't obstacles to work around, they're variables to engineer for. The plan has to work in your actual life.

04

The Person

Lasting change comes from becoming someone who can sustain the result. The goal isn't just a different body. It's a different relationship with your body, your habits and yourself.

In Practice

What this looks like with real clients.

The four layers don't operate in isolation. Each one supports the others, and working across all four simultaneously compounds results in a way isolated interventions can't match. Here are two anonymised examples.

The binge that wasn't about food

A client gained 15 kg in a single month during a period of extreme stress. When we unpacked it, what emerged was an inner voice running a specific script: "You already feel terrible, may as well feel even worse."

The eating wasn't impulsive. It was self-punishment. The intervention wasn't a meal plan. It was naming that voice, creating psychological distance from it, and building a list of alternative responses for when it showed up. The bingeing stopped. The weight came back off.

The restriction that wasn't about weight

A client was chronically under-eating. On the surface it looked like discipline. Underneath it, food was the one thing she had complete control over in a life shaped largely by external control: family, culture, a relationship that left little room for her own choices.

Trying to fix the eating in isolation would have been pointless. Understanding what it was doing for her unlocked everything. Better nutrition, better energy, better mood, reduced anxiety. One thread, pulled carefully.

Questions

About the method.

Is body psychology the same as therapy or clinical psychology?

No. Body psychology is a coaching methodology, not a clinical treatment. It's designed for people who are functional and capable but stuck in patterns they can't seem to break. If someone is dealing with a clinical eating disorder, serious mental health condition or complex trauma, the right support is clinical, and I'll say so clearly. If you're struggling to change despite knowing what to do, and ready to look honestly at what's in the way, this is designed for you.

How is this different from working with a personal trainer?

Most personal trainers work on the body. Some good ones address mindset as a support layer. Body psychology treats the psychological work as the foundation, not the add-on. Four years in the NHS, sitting with hundreds of patients and learning to identify what people aren't saying, is a different skill set to motivational coaching. The plan comes after the person is understood, not before.

Do I need to be struggling with my mental health to benefit from this?

No. Most clients are high-functioning people who perform well in other areas of life and genuinely can't understand why this one keeps defeating them. You don't need a diagnosis or a crisis. You just need to be honest that the issue isn't information, and be willing to look at what's actually going on.

How long before I see results?

Physical changes typically become visible within 8 to 12 weeks when the foundations are in place. The more significant shift, where the pattern of starting and stopping finally breaks, usually happens in the first few weeks once the psychological work begins. Most clients say that shift is what they were actually looking for.
